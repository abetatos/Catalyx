**Non-obvious finding:**

The market has repriced `cybersecurity_commercial` aggressively in 2026: CIBR (commercial-weighted) +32% YTD on CrowdStrike and Palo Alto revenue beats. `cybersecurity_defense` has not followed — CIBR's defense-adjacent holdings (Booz Allen, Leidos, SAIC) have lagged the commercial-cyber component by the width of the entire ETF's YTD return. This divergence is the mispricing.

The mechanism is structural, not accidental: defense-cyber revenue is recognised on contract milestones, not ARR renewals. The NATO 3.5% GDP budget commitment flows first into political announcement (done: Hague 2025 / June 2026 budget submissions), then into national budget appropriations (in progress: Q3–Q4 2026), then into contract award cadence (expected: H2 2026 – H1 2027), then into revenue recognition and earnings guidance (expected: FY2027). The market is pricing the commercial-cyber ARR cycle in real time; it is pricing the defense-cyber contract award cycle with a full budget cycle lag. The NATO 1.5% GDP cyber sub-target has never appeared as a line item in prior defense budgets — this is the first time a distinct cyber allocation exists. The DoD FY2026 cyber budget at $14.5B (CISA cut to $2.6B) confirms the DoD channel is insulated from civilian-agency headwinds. CMMC 2.0 compliance mandate affecting ~300,000 contractor organizations is still in early rollout — this is a secular consulting/certification revenue stream that does not yet appear in analyst models at Booz Allen or SAIC. These are the items the market has not priced.

`cybersecurity_commercial` holds momentum rank on enterprise ARR acceleration. `cybersecurity_defense` holds catalyst_alignment rank on the NATO contract cycle. They share ETF vehicles (ISPY.L, CIBR) but not thesis drivers — treating them as the same sector destroys alpha.
