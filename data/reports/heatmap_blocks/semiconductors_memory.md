**Non-obvious finding: the HBM4 share contest is the market's blind spot**

The mainstream narrative — "memory supercycle, buy DRAM" — has been fully priced into the re-rating (SK Hynix +230%, Micron +226% YTD). What the market has not yet differentiated is the HBM4 share redistribution underway in 2026. Samsung held only ~17% of HBM3E and was blocked from Nvidia's Blackwell platform pending qualification. In HBM4 for Vera Rubin, Samsung has reportedly recovered to ~20–25% share and passed Nvidia's qualification — this is a non-trivial shift in earnings mix because HBM carries 3–5x the ASP of conventional DRAM. The implication: Samsung's operating margins (depressed throughout HBM3E) are set to recover faster than Street models imply, while SK Hynix's per-unit economics face mild share dilution at the margin. Meanwhile, conventional DRAM prices are also surging (+90%+ QoQ in Q1) — the HBM wafer-cannibalization effect has tightened the commodity DRAM market far beyond what AI demand alone would justify. This cross-product tightening is not in sell-side HBM models and means the pricing upcycle is broader and more durable than the consensus "pure HBM story" implies. The exit trigger remains the same — first deceleration in contract-price QoQ growth — but the cycle may be stickier than the 2017–2018 analog because conventional DRAM is now also pricing power-constrained, not just AI memory. The distinction from `semiconductors_design` is structural: this sector's P&L is driven by ASP × volume on a commodity product, not IP-licensing or service fees — so the boom-bust amplitude is ~3x that of fabless designers, and timing the cycle matters far more than for `semiconductors_foundry` (TSMC, whose returns are tied to wafer-service fee expansion and are comparatively stable).
